## 📖 Core Concept
Digital certificates are digitally-signed electronic documents that bind a public key to a specific identity (a person, server, workstation, or device). They are the foundation of trust in Public Key Infrastructure (PKI).

* **Function:** They provide a mechanism to verify identity and facilitate secure, encrypted communication over untrusted networks.
* **Standard:** Most digital certificates utilize the **X.509** standard.
* **Contents:** A certificate includes the owner's name, organization, public key, and details about the issuing Certificate Authority (CA).
* **Trust Model:** Trust is established through a hierarchical "Chain of Trust," where a root CA vouches for the identity of entities.

## 🛠️ Hardware & Devices
* **Web Servers:** The primary endpoint for digital certificates to enable HTTPS/TLS.
* **Workstations/Devices:** End-user devices that may hold certificates for authentication.

## 📜 Protocols & Standards
* **X.509:** The standard format for public key certificates used in PKI.
* **AES (Advanced Encryption Standard):** A symmetric encryption algorithm used for bulk data transfer once a secure tunnel is established via the certificate's public key.
* **HTTPS/TLS:** The underlying protocols that utilize digital certificates to establish secure sessions.

## 🔑 Key Terminology
* **Digital Certificate:** An electronic document binding a public key to an identity.
* **Wildcard Certificate:** A certificate that uses a wildcard character (e.g., `*.diontraining.com`) to cover all subdomains under a single domain, simplifying management.
* **SAN (Subject Alternative Name):** A field in a certificate that allows a single certificate to support multiple different domain names (e.g., `diontraining.com` and `jasondion.com`).
* **Single-Sided Certificate:** Authentication where only the server presents a certificate to the client; the client does not need a certificate.
* **Dual-Sided Certificate:** Authentication where both the server and the client must present certificates to each other (higher security, higher processing overhead).
* **Self-Signed Certificate:** A certificate signed by the entity itself rather than a trusted third-party CA. Used for testing/internal systems; triggers browser warnings.
* **Third-Party Certificate:** A certificate issued and signed by a trusted CA (e.g., Verisign, CloudFlare).
* **Root of Trust (Chain of Trust):** The hierarchical validation path where a certificate is trusted because it was signed by a CA that is itself trusted.
* **CA (Certificate Authority):** A trusted third-party entity that issues, manages, and signs digital certificates.
* **RA (Registration Authority):** An entity that verifies the identity of a requestor and forwards the request to the CA for certificate issuance.
* **CSR (Certificate Signing Request):** A block of encoded text generated by the requester containing their public key and identity information, sent to the CA to request a certificate.
* **CRL (Certificate Revocation List):** A publicly accessible list maintained by a CA of certificates that have been revoked before their expiration date (e.g., due to compromise).
* **Key Escrow Agent:** A secure entity or system that holds a copy of a user's private key to prevent data loss.
* **Key Recovery Agent:** Specialized software/process used to restore lost or corrupted keys from a backup.

## 🔄 How It Works (Processes)
1. **Certificate Request:** The user/server generates a **CSR** (containing the public key) and sends it to the **RA**.
2. **Validation:** The **RA** verifies the identity and forwards the request to the **CA**.
3. **Issuance:** The **CA** signs the certificate and returns it to the user.
4. **Handshake/Encryption:**
 * A client connects to a server.
 * The server sends its certificate.
 * The client verifies the certificate against the **Root of Trust**.
 * The client encrypts a "shared secret" using the server's public key.
 * The server decrypts it with its private key.
 * Both parties use the shared secret for **AES** symmetric bulk encryption.
5. **Revocation:** If a key is compromised, the CA adds the certificate serial number to the **CRL**. Clients check this list before trusting a certificate.

## 📊 Comparisons & Key Differences

| Feature| Wildcard Certificate| SAN Certificate|
| :---| :---| :---|
| **Scope**| All subdomains of one domain| Multiple different domains|
| **Management**| Easier (one cert for all subdomains)| More complex (requires field modification)|
| **Use Case**| `www.site.com`, `mail.site.com`| `site.com`, `other-site.com`|

## ⚠️ Exam Tips & Important Notes
* **Wildcard Risk:** If a wildcard certificate is compromised, *all* subdomains are compromised.
* **Private Key Security:** The private key is **never** sent to the CA during the CSR process.
* **Key Escrow:** Always implement "separation of duties" (e.g., two administrators required) when accessing escrowed keys.
* **Root CA Compromise:** If a Root CA is compromised, every certificate it ever issued is invalid and must be revoked/reissued.
* **Browser Warnings:** Self-signed certificates will always trigger browser warnings because they lack third-party verification.
