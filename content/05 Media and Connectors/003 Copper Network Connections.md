## 📖 Core Concept
Copper network connections rely on specific physical connectors to interface with various types of cabling (twisted pair or coaxial). The cable itself may be identical in some instances (e.g., Cat 5e), but the **connector** determines the application (voice vs. data) and the physical interface (wall jack size/format). Connectors are generally categorized by standardized naming conventions: **RJ** (Registered Jack) for twisted pair and **RG** (Radio Guide) for coaxial.

## 🛠️ Hardware & Devices
* **RJ-11 Connector:** Used for telephone equipment.
* **RJ-45 Connector:** Used for Ethernet networking (computers, switches, routers).
* **F-Type Connector:** Screw-on connector for coaxial cable (TV, satellite, cable internet).
* **BNC (Bayonet Neill-Concealment) Connector:** Push-and-twist connector for professional video/RF.
* **Cabling Types:**
 * **Twisted Pair:** Cat 5, 5e, 6, 6a, 7, 8.
 * **Coaxial:** RG-6 (modern standard), RG-59 (legacy/CCTV).

## 📜 Protocols & Standards
* **RJ-X (Registered Jack):** A standardized telecommunication network interface for connecting voice and data equipment to a service provider.
* **RG-X (Radio Guide):** A standard for coaxial cable specifications.
* **6P2C (Six-Position, Two-Conductor):** The standard configuration for RJ-11.
* **8P8C (Eight-Position, Eight-Conductor):** The standard configuration for RJ-45.

## 🔑 Key Terminology
* **Registered Jack (RJ):** A standardized physical network interface for connecting telecommunications equipment.
* **Radio Guide (RG):** A series of standards used to classify coaxial cables based on their physical and electrical properties.
* **Twisted Pair:** A type of copper cabling where pairs of wires are twisted together to reduce electromagnetic interference (EMI).
* **Coaxial Cable:** A type of copper cable consisting of a center conductor, an insulating layer, a metallic shield, and an outer jacket.
* **Bayonet Neill-Concealment (BNC):** A miniature quick-connect/disconnect RF connector used for coaxial cable.
* **Dielectric:** The insulating material between the center conductor and the shield in a coaxial cable.

## 🔄 How It Works (Processes)
* **RJ-11 Termination:** Uses 2 of 6 available slots to carry voice signals.
* **RJ-45 Termination:** Uses all 8 pins to support high-speed Ethernet data transmission.
* **F-Type Connection:** A threaded, screw-on mechanism that ensures a secure connection for coaxial cables.
* **BNC Connection:** Features two bayonet lugs on the female connector; requires a push and a quarter-turn to lock the male connector into the female jack.

## 📊 Comparisons & Key Differences

| Feature| RJ-11| RJ-45|
| :---| :---| :---|
| **Configuration**| 6P2C| 8P8C|
| **Primary Use**| Telephone/Voice| Ethernet Data|
| **Data Speed**| Low (Not for high-speed data)| High (Supports Cat 5-8)|

| Feature| RG-6| RG-59|
| :---| :---| :---|
| **Application**| Modern TV, Cable Internet| Legacy TV, CCTV|
| **Shielding**| Better/Thicker| Thinner/Older|
| **Performance**| Higher frequency/longer distance| Lower frequency/shorter distance|

## ⚠️ Exam Tips & Important Notes
* **Connector vs. Cable:** Remember that the same cable (e.g., Cat 5e) can be terminated with different connectors (RJ-11 vs. RJ-45) for different purposes.
* **BNC Misconception:** While often called "British Naval Connector," the correct technical term is **Bayonet Neill-Concealment**.
* **CCTV Usage:** If you see a question about legacy or low-bandwidth video surveillance (CCTV), think **RG-59**.
* **Modern Standard:** **RG-6** is the current standard for high-speed cable internet and digital TV.
* **RJ-45 Versatility:** RJ-45 is the de facto standard for modern enterprise and residential Ethernet.
