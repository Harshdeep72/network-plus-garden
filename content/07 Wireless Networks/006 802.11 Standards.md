## 📖 Core Concept
The **802.11 standard** is a set of specifications developed by the **IEEE** (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers) that defines the technology used for Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN) communications, commonly known as Wi-Fi. These standards have evolved over time to provide higher data transfer speeds, increased reliability, and better frequency management.

Key factors to master for each standard include:
* **Frequency Band:** The radio spectrum utilized (2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, or 6 GHz).
* **Maximum Speed:** The theoretical throughput limit.
* **Maximum Distance/Range:** The effective coverage area of a single access point.

## 🛠️ Hardware & Devices
* **Wireless Access Point (WAP):** The central device that connects wireless clients to the network.
* **Wireless Network Interface Card (NIC):** The hardware inside a computer/laptop that allows it to communicate via wireless standards.
* **Antennas:** Used in MIMO configurations to send/receive data streams simultaneously.
* **Household Devices (2.4 GHz interference):** Security cameras, walkie-talkies, baby monitors, and microwaves.

## 📜 Protocols & Standards
* **802.11a (Wireless a):**
 * **Frequency:** 5 GHz
 * **Speed:** Up to 54 Mbps
 * **Range:** ~35 meters
* **802.11b (Wireless b):**
 * **Frequency:** 2.4 GHz
 * **Speed:** Up to 11 Mbps
 * **Range:** ~140 meters
* **802.11g (Wireless g):**
 * **Frequency:** 2.4 GHz
 * **Speed:** Up to 54 Mbps
 * **Range:** ~140 meters
* **802.11n (Wireless n / Wi-Fi 4):**
 * **Frequency:** 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz (Dual-band)
 * **Speed:** Up to 600 Mbps
 * **Range:** ~35 meters (5 GHz) to ~70 meters (2.4 GHz)
* **802.11ac (Wireless ac / Wi-Fi 5):**
 * **Frequency:** 5 GHz (Official standard)
 * **Speed:** Up to 6.9 Gbps
* **802.11ax (Wireless ax / Wi-Fi 6 / Wi-Fi 6E):**
 * **Frequency:** 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and 6 GHz (Wi-Fi 6E)
 * **Speed:** Up to 9.6 Gbps

## 🔑 Key Terminology
* **IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers):** The organization responsible for defining the 802.11 standards.
* **MIMO (Multiple-Input and Multiple-Output):** A technology that uses multiple antennas to send and receive data simultaneously, increasing throughput.
* **MU-MIMO (Multiple-User, Multiple-Input, Multiple-Output):** An evolution of MIMO that allows an access point to communicate with multiple users simultaneously, functioning more like a network switch than a hub.
* **Backwards Compatibility:** The ability of a newer standard to communicate with devices using older standards (e.g., 802.11ax supporting 802.11b/g/n/ac).
* **Dual-Band:** A device capable of operating on two different frequency bands (e.g., 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz) simultaneously.

## 🔄 How It Works (Processes)
* **MIMO Multiplexing:** Data is split across multiple antennas at the source and multiplexed back into a single data stream at the destination.
* **MU-MIMO Switching:** Unlike standard MIMO (which acts like a hub, switching between users), MU-MIMO allows the access point to handle multiple user requests at once, reducing congestion and collisions.
* **Hybrid Access Points:** To achieve backwards compatibility, manufacturers include separate radios for different frequencies (e.g., a 2.4 GHz radio for legacy devices and a 5 GHz radio for modern high-speed devices).

## ⚠️ Exam Tips & Important Notes
* **Ignore Marketing Claims:** Retail packaging often labels "Wireless ac" as dual-band. **On the exam, 802.11ac is strictly 5 GHz.** If a device supports both, it is because it has two separate radios (one for 802.11ac and one for 802.11n).
* **Dual-Band Standards:** Only **802.11n** and **802.11ax** are officially dual-band per the IEEE standard.
* **Troubleshooting:** If a device cannot connect, check for frequency mismatches. An 802.11b card cannot connect to an 802.11ac network because 802.11b is 2.4 GHz and 802.11ac is 5 GHz.
* **Speed vs. Distance:** Generally, as speed increases, the effective range of a single access point decreases.
