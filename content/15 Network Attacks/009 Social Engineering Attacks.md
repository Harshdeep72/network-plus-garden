## 📖 Core Concept
**Social Engineering**: The act of manipulating individuals into revealing confidential information or performing actions that compromise the security of a system or organization.
* **The Human Element**: Social engineering targets the "weakest link" in security—the end user—to bypass robust technical controls (e.g., encryption, firewalls).
* **Philosophy**: It is often easier to "hack the human" than to brute-force a complex, secure system.
* **Prevention**: The primary defense is consistent, high-quality **end-user cybersecurity training** to increase awareness and skepticism.

## 🛠️ Hardware & Devices
* **Access Badge/Reader**: Physical security devices used to control entry to sensitive areas (e.g., server rooms).
* **Cross-cut Shredder**: A device used to destroy sensitive paper documents into small particles, preventing information recovery from trash.
* **Locked Trash Containers**: Physical security containers used to store sensitive waste, accessible only by authorized personnel and waste management services.

## 🔑 Key Terminology
* **Phishing**: A broad attack where an attacker sends fraudulent emails (often mimicking legitimate brands like PayPal) to trick users into clicking links or providing credentials.
* **Spear Phishing**: A targeted phishing attack directed at a specific individual or group, often using gathered intelligence (e.g., a list of bank customers) to increase credibility.
* **Whaling**: A specialized form of spear phishing that targets high-level executives (CEO, CFO, CIO, etc.) within an organization.
* **Tailgating**: An unauthorized person following an authorized person into a secure area without the authorized person's knowledge.
* **Piggybacking**: Similar to tailgating, but occurs with the authorized person's knowledge or consent (e.g., holding the door open for someone whose hands are full).
* **Shoulder Surfing**: Using direct observation (looking over a shoulder or glancing at a screen) to steal authentication credentials or sensitive data.
* **Eavesdropping**: Using direct observation (listening) to overhear private conversations and gain confidential information.
* **Dumpster Diving**: Scavenging through trash or recycling bins to find sensitive documents, phone lists, or organizational information.

## 🔄 How It Works (Processes)
* **Phishing Workflow**:
 1. Attacker crafts an email mimicking a trusted entity (logo, formatting).
 2. User clicks a malicious link.
 3. User is redirected to a fake login page controlled by the attacker.
 4. User enters credentials, which are captured by the attacker.
* **Dumpster Diving Prevention**:
 1. Identify sensitive documents.
 2. Destroy via **cross-cut shredder** OR secure in **locked trash containers**.

## 📊 Comparisons & Key Differences

| Attack Type| Target Scope| Knowledge/Consent of Victim|
| :---| :---| :---|
| **Phishing**| Broad/Mass| Unaware|
| **Spear Phishing**| Targeted/Specific| Unaware|
| **Whaling**| Executives| Unaware|
| **Tailgating**| Physical Access| Unaware|
| **Piggybacking**| Physical Access| Aware/Consenting|

## ⚠️ Exam Tips & Important Notes
* **The Human Factor**: Always remember that social engineering is about manipulating people, not software.
* **Phishing Success Rates**: Even with poor grammar or bad logos, phishing campaigns can still achieve 30–40% success rates. High-quality campaigns can reach 60–70%.
* **Tailgating vs. Piggybacking**: The key differentiator is **consent**. If the victim knows they are letting someone in, it is piggybacking. If they are unaware, it is tailgating.
* **Prevention**: Training is the most effective tool against all forms of social engineering.
