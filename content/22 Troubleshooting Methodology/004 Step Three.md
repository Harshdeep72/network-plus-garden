## 📖 Core Concept
Step Three of the CompTIA troubleshooting methodology is **Test the Theory to Determine the Cause**.

* **Purpose:** This is the first stage in the methodology where the technician actively performs configurations or physical changes to the system to validate the hypothesis formed in Step Two.
* **The Iterative Nature:** Troubleshooting is often a cycle. If a theory is tested and proven incorrect, the technician must return to Step Two to establish a new theory and then return to Step Three to test it.
* **Outcome-Driven:** The goal is to move from a "probable cause" to a "confirmed cause," which then dictates the next steps for resolution.

## 🛠️ Hardware & Devices
* **Computer Tower:** Used as the primary example for troubleshooting power issues.
* **Wall Outlet:** The power source for the hardware; tested for voltage output.
* **Voltmeter:** A diagnostic tool used to measure electrical potential (voltage) to confirm if an outlet is providing the expected 120V AC.
* **Power Supply (PSU):** The internal component responsible for converting AC power to DC power for computer components; identified as a potential point of failure.

## 🔑 Key Terminology
* **Troubleshooting Methodology:** A standardized, step-by-step process used to identify, analyze, and resolve technical issues efficiently.
* **Theory:** A hypothesis or educated guess regarding the root cause of a technical problem, based on observations made in Step One.
* **Escalation:** The process of transferring a support ticket or issue to a higher tier of support or a different department when the current technician lacks the authority, skills, or resources to resolve the problem.
* **Tiered Support Structure:** A hierarchical system for organizing technical support:
 * **Tier 1:** Entry-level support handling basic, common issues.
 * **Tier 2:** Intermediate support handling more complex technical problems.
 * **Tier 3:** Expert-level support (Subject Matter Experts, System Admins, Cybersecurity experts) handling the most difficult or specialized issues.

## 🔄 How It Works (Processes)
The process of Step Three follows a logical flow:
1. **Execute Test:** Perform a specific action to verify the theory (e.g., checking if a cable is plugged in).
2. **Evaluate Result:**
 * If the test confirms the theory, proceed to resolution.
 * If the test fails to confirm the theory, return to Step Two to create a new theory.
3. **Determine Resolution/Escalation:** Once the cause is confirmed, decide if you can fix it or if it must be escalated.

**The Four Possible Outcomes of Step Three:**
1. **Theory Confirmed:** The cause is identified; proceed to fix the issue.
2. **Theory Not Confirmed:** The hypothesis was wrong; return to Step Two to develop a new theory.
3. **Escalation Required:** The problem is identified, but the technician lacks the **authority** (e.g., expensive parts), **skills** (e.g., complex server configuration), or **access** (e.g., security/malware incidents) to fix it.
4. **Stuck/Unknown:** The technician cannot identify the cause after multiple theories; escalate to a higher tier of support.

## 📊 Comparisons & Key Differences
| Outcome| Action Required|
| :---| :---|
| **Theory Confirmed**| Proceed to resolution (fix/replace).|
| **Theory Not Confirmed**| Return to Step Two (re-evaluate).|
| **Escalation**| Transfer to higher tier or specialized team.|

## ⚠️ Exam Tips & Important Notes
* **No Changes Before Step Three:** The instructor emphasizes that you should NOT perform configurations or changes to the system until you reach Step Three.
* **Escalation is Valid:** Do not view escalation as a failure. It is a standard part of the troubleshooting process when you lack the necessary permissions or expertise.
* **Corporate Policy:** Always consider organizational policies regarding hardware replacement and security protocols when deciding whether to fix or escalate.
* **The "Loop":** Remember that troubleshooting is not always linear; you will frequently loop between Step Two and Step Three until the root cause is found.
