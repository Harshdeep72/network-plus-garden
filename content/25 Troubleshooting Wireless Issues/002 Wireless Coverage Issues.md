## 📖 Core Concept
Wireless coverage refers to the physical area around a wireless transmitter where the signal strength is sufficient for a wireless device to maintain a reliable connection. When a device moves outside this area, the signal degrades or drops entirely.

* **Wireless Site Survey:** A process used to determine the coverage area of a network.
* **Heat Map:** A visual representation generated by a site survey.
 * **Green:** Highest signal strength.
 * **Yellow/Orange:** Moderate signal strength.
 * **Red:** Weakest signal strength.
* **Factors Affecting Coverage:**
 1. **Transmitter Power:** The amount of power the access point (AP) sends out.
 2. **Antenna Size:** The gain of the antenna used.
 3. **Physical Obstructions:** Signals weaken as they pass through floors, ceilings, and walls.

## 🛠️ Hardware & Devices
* **Wireless Access Point (WAP/AP):** A device that allows wireless-capable devices to connect to a wired network.
* **Wireless Repeater:** A Layer 1 device containing two radios. It receives a signal from an existing network, boosts it, and retransmits it to extend coverage.
* **Antennas:** Hardware used to transmit/receive signals. Replacing a lower-gain antenna (e.g., 5 dB) with a higher-gain antenna (e.g., 9 dB) can effectively double the range under optimal conditions.
* **Ethernet Twisted-Pair Cable:** Used to connect multiple WAPs to a wired backbone to create an Extended Service Set (ESS).
* **Wireless Mesh Systems:** Modern systems that combine the functionality of repeaters and access points into a single ecosystem, using internal radios to communicate with each other without requiring dedicated ethernet backhaul to every node.

## 📜 Protocols & Standards
* **ESS (Extended Service Set):** A configuration where multiple WAPs are connected to the same wired network (usually via Ethernet) to provide seamless roaming and coverage across a large area.
* **FCC (Federal Communications Commission):** The regulatory body that sets the maximum power output limits for wireless transmitters to prevent interference and ensure safety.

## 🔑 Key Terminology
* **Coverage:** The physical area where a wireless device has sufficient signal strength to function.
* **RSSI (Received Signal Strength Indicator):** A measurement of the power present in a received radio signal, measured from the perspective of the **client device**. Measured in **decibels (dB)**.
* **EIRP (Equivalent Isotropically Radiated Power):** The total power radiated by an antenna. Measured from the perspective of the **access point**. Measured in **dBi (decibels relative to an isotropic antenna)**.
* **Roaming:** The ability of a wireless client to automatically switch between different access points as it moves through a facility to maintain a strong connection.
* **Signal Booster:** A device used to increase the power of a wireless signal.

## 🔄 How It Works (Processes)
* **Wireless Repeater Operation:**
 1. Radio A receives the signal from the primary WAP (e.g., at -70 RSSI).
 2. The device boosts the signal.
 3. Radio B retransmits the signal at full strength (e.g., at -30 RSSI) to the client.
* **ESS Configuration:**
 1. Multiple WAPs are installed in different locations.
 2. Each WAP is connected via Ethernet to the network.
 3. Each WAP is configured to operate on a different channel to avoid interference.
 4. Clients "roam" between WAPs automatically based on signal strength.

## 📊 Comparisons & Key Differences
| Feature| Wireless Repeater| Extended Service Set (ESS)|
| :---| :---| :---|
| **Backhaul**| Wireless (uses internal radios)| Wired (Ethernet)|
| **Performance**| Lower (bandwidth halved due to repeating)| Higher (full bandwidth via wired connection)|
| **Complexity**| Simple (plug and play)| Higher (requires cabling)|

## ⚠️ Exam Tips & Important Notes
* **Measurement Perspective:** Remember that **RSSI** is measured from the **client**, while **EIRP/dBi** is measured from the **AP**.
* **Antenna Math:** Replacing a 5 dB antenna with a 9 dB antenna can effectively **double the range** under the right conditions.
* **Regulatory Limits:** You cannot simply increase transmitter power indefinitely; you are strictly limited by **FCC regulations** regarding maximum power output.
* **Repeater Limitation:** A repeater is a Layer 1 device; it does not "understand" the data, it simply repeats the signal it receives.
